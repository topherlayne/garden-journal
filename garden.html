Session 1: Plant this week (April 7–13)

Cool-season crops that handle frost and prefer cooler soil.

Before you plant: Rake the beds smooth (not the path). Push extra soil depth toward the south end where tomatoes will go. If you bought extra compost bags, mound that soil onto the carrot and beet squares specifically. Water deeply and let it soak for an hour. Damp, not muddy.

Bok choy

Depth
¼ inch
Grid
4 per sq ft (6” apart)
Method
2–3 seeds per spot, thin to 1

Spring crop that bolts in summer heat. Harvest before late May. Snip thinnings at soil level. Replant in September for fall.

Carrots (short variety)

Depth
¼ inch, barely covered
Grid
16 per sq ft (3” apart, 4×4)
Germination
10–14 days (slow, don't panic)

Get Chantenay or Paris Market, not long varieties. Keep soil consistently moist. Damp burlap over the section helps. Thin once tops are a couple inches tall. Mound extra compost here if you bought it.

Beets

Depth
½ inch
Grid
9 per sq ft (4” apart, 3×3)

Each "seed" is a cluster of 2–4 seeds. Thin to one per spot at 2 inches tall. Eat thinnings as salad greens. 55–65 days to harvest.

Daikon radish

Depth
½ inch
Grid
9 per sq ft (4” apart, 3×3)

Roots get 12+ inches long and will push through the softening cardboard on their own. Great for kimchi and quick pickles. 50–60 days.

Green onion (bunching)

Depth
¼ inch
Grid
16 per sq ft (scatter thickly)

Easiest thing in the garden. Scatter densely, cover, water. Harvest at pencil-thick, or cut tops and let them regrow.

Cilantro

Depth
¼ inch
Grid
9 per sq ft

Bolts fast in heat. 4–6 weeks of harvest per sowing. Let some go to seed for coriander. Resow every 3 weeks. Stop in summer, restart September.

Dill

Depth
¼ inch
Grid
1 per sq ft (gets 2–3 ft tall)

Plant at the back of the herb zone. Attracts swallowtail butterflies. Let seed heads drop for next year's volunteers.

Zinnias

Depth
¼ inch
Grid
4 per sq ft (6” apart)

Won't do much until soil warms. They'll explode with summer heat. Pick flowers regularly to keep them producing.

Marigolds (French type)

Depth
¼ inch
Grid
4 per sq ft (6” apart)

Look good and repel whiteflies and nematodes. Get Tagetes patula (French), not African marigolds.

Chamomile

Depth
Press into surface, needs light to germinate
Grid
4 per sq ft

Stays low (12–18 inches), white blooms with yellow centers. Makes tea. Self-sows gently without taking over. Tuck in alongside zinnias in the sign row.

Session 2: Plant after April 15–20

Warm-season crops that die on contact with frost. Wait until nighttime temps are reliably above 50°F. Harden off transplants: outside during the day, inside at night, for about 10 days.

Three things not to forget:
1. Soak okra seeds overnight before planting.
2. Bury tomatoes deep — 2/3 of the plant underground.
3. Put tomato supports in at planting time, not later.

Tomatoes (heirloom ×2, cherry ×2)

Grid
1 per 4 sq ft (24” apart, each gets a 2×2 block)
Where
South end of both beds

Bury deep. Strip lower leaves and bury 2/3 of the plant. A 12-inch transplant should have only 4 inches above ground. Install cage or stake at planting. Side-dress with 10-10-10 when you see small green fruits.

Cucumbers (×2)

Grid
2 per sq ft on trellis
From seed
1 inch deep, 2–3 seeds per hole, thin to 1

Train up the trellis along the south edge. Heavy drinkers in July. Harvest at 6–8 inches.

Okra (×4)

Depth
1 inch (from seed)
Grid
1 per 2 sq ft (12–18” apart)

Soak seeds overnight. Won't grow until soil hits 65°F+. Harvest pods at 3–4 inches. Check every other day. Gets 4–6 feet tall.

Jalapeño (×2)

Grid
1 per sq ft

Slow to start, takes off in heat. Left until red, they're hotter and sweeter. Standard transplant depth.

Shishito (×2)

Grid
1 per sq ft

Harvest bright green at 3–4 inches. About 1 in every 8–10 is spicy. Perfect blistered in a skillet with oil and salt.

Banana pepper (×2)

Grid
1 per sq ft

Ready when yellow-green, about 6 inches. For pickling, harvest slightly under-ripe for crunch and tang.

Basil

Depth
Press into surface, barely cover
Grid
1 per sq ft

Most cold-sensitive herb. Wait until nights above 55°F. Pinch off flower buds. Cut above a leaf pair to encourage branching.

Floating row cover

Your defense against cabbage moths for the brassica zone. You need two separate pieces, one for each bed's brassica section. Each piece is roughly 6 × 7 feet. Do not span across the path, or you can't walk to the south end without lifting it every time.

Drape loosely over the plants. Seal all four edges of each piece: tuck into soil along the curbing side, weight down along the path edge and north/south boundaries with rocks or bricks. Leave on for the entire crop life. It lets in light, water, and air.

When to pick

The most common beginner mistake is harvesting too late. Most vegetables taste better slightly young. Here's how to know when each crop is ready.

Bok choy

Ready: 45–50 days from sowing

Harvest when heads are 8–10 inches tall with thick white stems. The real signal: if you see a stalk pushing up from the center, that's bolting. Harvest immediately, even if the plant seems small. Pull the whole plant, roots and all. Sow the replacement the same day.

Carrots

Ready: ~60 days for short varieties

Pull one test carrot. If the shoulder (where root meets soil surface) is about ¾ inch across for short varieties, they're ready. If still skinny, give it two more weeks. Big leafy tops don't mean big roots.

Beets

Ready: 55–65 days

The top of the root pushes above the soil line. Harvest when the visible shoulder is 1.5–2 inches across. Don't leave them too long or they get woody inside. Pull a test beet to check.

Daikon radish

Ready: 50–60 days

The root pushes up out of the soil so you can see how thick it's getting. Harvest at 2–3 inches diameter. Left too long, they crack and get pithy. Even a forked one makes good kimchi.

Green onion

Ready: any time they're pencil-thick

There's no wrong time. Pull whole onions, or cut the green tops and let them regrow. They just keep going.

Cilantro

Ready: ongoing until it bolts

Harvest outer leaves any time the plant is 6+ inches tall. When a tall flower stalk shoots up from the center, the leaves turn bitter. That's your cue to pull and resow. Let one or two plants go to seed for free coriander.

Chamomile

Ready: when flowers are fully open

Pick the flowers when petals are fully spread and the yellow center is bright. Dry them for tea. The more you pick, the more it produces. Harvest in the morning after dew dries.

Tomatoes

Ready: full color + slight give when squeezed

Cherry tomatoes should pop off the vine with a gentle tug. If you have to yank, wait another day. Heirlooms: full color, slight give, easy detach. You can also pick at the first blush of color and ripen indoors on the counter.

Cucumbers

Ready: 6–8 inches, dark green, firm

Check vines every other day in peak season. A cucumber goes from perfect to overripe in 48 hours. If it's turning yellow or getting fat and puffy, it's past prime. Leaving overripe fruit on the vine tells the plant to stop producing.

Peppers (all types)

Ready: depends on type

Jalapeños: pick green at 3–4 inches, or leave until red for more heat and sweetness. Shishitos: pick bright green at 3–4 inches. Banana: pick yellow-green at 6 inches. All peppers twist off with a slight upward pull, or snip with scissors to avoid snapping the branch.

Okra

Ready: pods at 3–4 inches long — check every other day

This is the crop that punishes you for missing a day. Anything over 5 inches is getting woody and tough. Cut the pod off with a knife or scissors. Once plants start producing, you're checking every 48 hours.

Cabbage

Ready: when head is firm and solid

Squeeze the head. If it feels solid and tight, it's ready. If it's still loose and leafy, give it more time. Cut the head off at the base, leaving the outer leaves and root. Some varieties will sprout smaller bonus heads from the stump.

Kale

Ready: ongoing — pick outer leaves

Never harvest the whole plant. Pick the largest outer leaves from the bottom up, leaving the growing center and at least 5–6 inner leaves. The plant keeps producing new leaves from the top all season. Gets sweeter after a light frost in fall.

Succession sowing + replacements

Some crops finish fast and free up space for a new round. Here's when to resow and what to put in empty squares.

Cilantro: Resow every 3 weeks from April through early June. Stop when daytime temps stay above 85°F. Start again in September.

Bok choy: Sow once now (spring), harvest by late May, replace the square with a warm-season crop (more okra, bush beans, or basil). Sow bok choy again in August for fall harvest.

Carrots + beets: Sow now, harvest June/July. Sow a second round in August for fall.

Daikon: Same as carrots. Spring round now, fall round in August/September.

Green onion: Sow once and keep cutting. They regrow. If you want more, scatter seeds in any bare square whenever you notice one.

Watering

At planting

Soak the top 4–5 inches completely. Settles soil around seeds and roots, softens the cardboard below.

First two weeks

Water daily or every other day. Surface stays moist but shouldn't puddle. Gentle spray only.

After establishment

Every 2–3 days in spring. Daily in peak July–August heat. Water in the morning. Direct at soil, not leaves.

Diagnosing trouble: Afternoon wilting on a hot day is normal. Still wilted in the morning? Water immediately. Yellow lower leaves + damp soil = nitrogen deficiency from the mulch component, not a water problem.

Fertilizer plan

The garden mix starts with decent nutrition, but the mulch component ties up nitrogen as it breaks down.

Weeks 1–4
Do nothing. Watch for yellowing on tomatoes and brassicas.
Weeks 4–6
Side-dress heavy feeders (tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, okra, cabbage, kale) with blood meal or 10-10-10. Small handful 3–4 inches from stem, scratch in, water.
At fruit set
Side-dress tomatoes and peppers again when small green fruits appear.
Root crops
No extra nitrogen. Bone meal only for phosphorus. Too much N = big tops, tiny roots.
Herbs
Don't fertilize. More flavor when slightly stressed.

Pest watch

Cabbage moths

Target: cabbage, kale, bok choy

Small white butterflies whose caterpillars eat holes in everything. Floating row cover is the fix. If caterpillars get through, BT spray (Thuricide or Dipel) kills them without harming anything else.

Tomato hornworm

Target: tomatoes

Fat green caterpillars, 3–4 inches, perfectly camouflaged. Hand-pick into soapy water. If one is covered in white cocoons, leave it — parasitic wasp eggs.

Cucumber beetles

Target: cucumbers

Small striped/spotted yellow-green beetles. Spread bacterial wilt (no cure). Neem oil early season, hand-pick. Sudden vine wilt = pull the plant.

Aphids

Target: everything

Tiny soft-bodied insects on new growth. Hard spray of water knocks them off. Insecticidal soap if persistent. Ladybugs will find them.

Fire ants

Target: your hands

You treated before clearing. Monitor for new mounds. Spinosad bait on new mounds. Don't let one establish in the bed.
